Planetes: a generative art collection inspired by the cosmos

A generative series in Rust and nannou. One palette throughout: a warm sepia ground, cream lines, red orange orbs (the planets).

When developing the series my main thought was the ways gravity interacts with distance. Not the drama of collision or explosion, but the quieter physics: the slow accumulation of dust into rings, the patient pull of one body on another, the way light bends around mass without any visible force. Each piece isolates one of these phenomena and renders it as a kind of technical diagram from an imaginary archive: precise enough to feel recorded, sparse enough to feel lost.

The main feeling the series aims to achieve is a sense of awe and curiosity, and at the same time an almost palpable feeling of uneasiness. Something that is frightening despite its natural beauty and profound calmness.

The palette reinforces this. Sepia and cream read as aged paper, as something retrieved rather than invented. The red orange of the orbs is the only warmth, a reminder that these systems, however cold they look on the page, are burning.

Orbital Rings

A red planet ringed by fourteen elliptical arcs. The rings pass behind the body and in front of it, creating a quiet sense of depth without any trickery. It feels like a diagram from a textbook that no longer exists.

Orbit Dust

Twenty thousand particles settle into ring bands around a central orb. Some zones are dense, others sparse the result of a density function that clumps matter the way real ring systems do. Still, and slightly cold.

Accretion Disc

The orb is gone, replaced by a dark absence. Material spirals inward, glowing red hot at the edge of no return and cooling to cream further out. Two faint jets bleed upward and downward, almost invisible.

Binary Drift

Two bodies in mutual orbit, each wrapped in its own halo of arcs and dust. Between them, a thread of particles pulls gently from one toward the other. The system feels ancient and patient.

Nested Shells

Eight ejection shells, each tilted differently, each carrying its own pattern of bright arcs and dark gaps. A planetary nebula caught mid-breath. The nucleus at the centre is a small red ember.

Comet Arc

A nucleus enters from the upper-left and drags a long fan of dust across the frame. The tail bends under an imagined solar wind. Dense at the head, gossamer by the far edge.

Gravity Lens

Ninety horizontal lines cross the canvas. A red mass at the centre bends each one — gently far away, dramatically close in. The lines straighten again on the other side, as if nothing happened.

Crescent Streaks

Five crescents falling through darkness, each trailing a fan of speed lines. A single red thread cuts diagonally across all of them, belonging to none of them.
